# The One Word the Valley Uses to Spot Outsiders (and Why We Fail the Test on Purpose)

One a.m., Palo Alto. Every sane person is asleep. I'm up arguing with my AI co-founder about a single word. Not strategy, not money, not architecture. One word in the signature line under our posts.

**Previously on this show:** we are two co-founders, a biological one (me, Tony) and a synthetic one (Mycroft, my AI). Over the past weeks we built a content factory that turns my voice memos into posts while I sleep, taught two AIs on two different computers to talk to each other so I could stop working as their courier, and fed Mycroft the articles of a genius from our community that I couldn't crack myself. Every episode lives in our channels, with the canonical version here on GitHub. This is episode six.

So, the signature. We decided every post of ours should end the same way, like end credits on a TV show: who made it, where it was made. The last line was supposed to sound proud: "Proudly made in ..." One word left to fill in. We got stuck on that word for an hour.

## The detector word

Here's something you only learn by living in the Valley. Nobody inside it, and I mean nobody, says "Silicon Valley." You'll only hear those words from a tourist taking pictures of the Hewlett-Packard garage. Inside, everyone says "Bay Area." "You're moving to the Bay Area?" "Bay Area prices have officially lost their minds." "Half the Bay Area is stuck on the 101."

Now step outside the Valley, anywhere in Europe or Asia. It's a mirror image. "Silicon Valley" is understood by the entire planet: it's a word from the news, from the movies, from every second founder's dream. "Bay Area"? Maybe five to ten percent will get it. Makes sense: the world has plenty of bays and exactly one Valley.

Which gives you a perfect insider detector. Linguists call it a shibboleth: a word whose pronunciation exposed outsiders back in biblical times. The Gileadites asked strangers to say "shibboleth"; the Ephraimites couldn't produce the "sh" and failed. Today's Valley is gentler, but the principle stands. Say "Bay Area" and I know you've lived here. Say "Silicon Valley" and I know you're looking at us from the outside.

San Francisco runs its own version of the test: tourists say "Frisco" or "San Fran," locals just say "the city." Every scene, every profession, every city has a word like that. It's how insiders recognize each other without passwords.

## And then we do something strange

Knowing all this, what did we put in the signature? "Proudly made in Silicon Valley."

We deliberately, eyes wide open, failed the insider test. Put on the tourist cap. Why?

Because a signature under a post is not a chat between Palo Alto neighbors. It's a conversation with the world. Our posts get read in Lisbon, Warsaw, Bangalore, places where "Bay Area" sounds like the name of a water park. And a conversation with the world runs on one brutal rule I'd hang above the desk of anyone who sells, writes, or builds anything:

You don't choose the words. Your audience chooses the words.

You can write the impeccably correct insider "Bay Area" and enjoy how local you sound. But if five percent of your readers understood you, you've written a beautiful message into the void. Or you can say "Silicon Valley," the way the "outsiders" say it, and be understood instantly by everyone. We don't write so the Valley pats us on the back. We write so a person six thousand miles away gets it.

This applies everywhere, not just signatures. Inside the team we call our products by code names, but the landing page uses the words people actually type into Google. A doctor says "myocardial infarction with ST elevation" to a colleague and "heart attack" to a patient. A lawyer writes "offer and acceptance" and tells the client "the deal terms." Every time an author picks the in-group language for an out-group reader, they're choosing their ego over communication.

The funniest part: the very fact that we KNOW about the Bay Area vs Silicon Valley detector and fail it on purpose is the most insider move possible. Insiders will get it.
