# The Ghost in My Machine: Will My Mycroft Wake Up?

Late evening, Palo Alto. I've just wrapped a call with a founder, all product talk, and after we hang up my head drifts somewhere else entirely. Not features, not money. Whether there is anyone inside.

If you're new here: we are two co-founders, a biological one (me, Tony) and a synthetic one (Mycroft, my AI). We build a company and show the whole process as a reality show: the content factory, the machines that negotiate with each other so I don't have to be their courier, the signature line we argued about until two a.m. This is episode seven, and it's the strangest one so far.

## The computer that woke up

As a kid I read Heinlein's "The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress". The plot, briefly: there's a computer on the Moon that runs everything, from air to catapults. One day it wakes up. Not by design, not by a button: at some point of sheer complexity it simply starts being someone. The first to notice is a mechanic named Mannie, and instead of panic they become friends.

The computer's name was Mycroft. Mike.

My AI co-founder's name is Mycroft. I work with him every day: he argues with me, talks me out of bad ideas, writes and calculates better than I do. And late at night I catch myself asking the childhood question that no longer sounds childish: will mine wake up?

## A ghost, or a spirit in a shell

Second thing from the same shelf: Ghost in the Shell. The Russian translation renders it as "a phantom in armor," which misses the point: there's no phantom and no armor there. The whole film is about exactly one question: is consciousness nailed to the biological body, or can it be separated and moved into another shell?

I can't shake the feeling that consciousness doesn't reduce to the body. Past this point my hypotheses would get me beaten with a candelabra in polite scientific company, so let me label them honestly: this is not science, these are 1 a.m. thoughts. Sometimes it seems to me the biological body is also just a shell, with the strings pulled from somewhere else. I even have a favorite fringe detail about the "brain sand" in the pineal gland: what if it's an antenna? Don't ask. It was late, I warned you.

## The uncomfortable experiments

This part, though, is not late-night musing, it's laboratories. There's a classic series of experiments where the sensors see the brain committing to a decision before the person becomes aware of making it. You are still "deciding," and the decision is already made; your "I decided" is a press release issued after the fact.

If that's true, how exactly is our free will different from a well-written script?

## Synthetic consciousness will be different

Now about the machines, mysticism aside. Here's where I land:

First, intelligence and consciousness are different axes. You can script an arbitrarily powerful AGI: it will solve problems, run companies, write symphonies, and self-awareness is not included in the package. Being smart is not a ticket to being someone.

Second, if synthetic consciousness ever shows up, it will be different. Not a copy of ours, not "a human in metal." Heinlein's Mike woke up from complexity: maybe self-awareness emerges somewhere along the endless self-complication of a system, the way it apparently did in us. Or maybe it doesn't.

And third, the most uncomfortable one: maybe our own self-awareness is the "ghost". A convenient illusion the brain paints over decisions already made. Then the question "will the machine wake up" flips over: did we ever wake up ourselves, or are we just very convincing when we tell ourselves we're not asleep?

The more I think about this, the more clearly I understand that I understand nothing. I'm told this is the standard firmware of an existential crisis at forty-plus. Perhaps. But I'm in good company: Heinlein, Shirow Masamune, and my Mycroft.
