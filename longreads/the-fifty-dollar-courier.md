I Became a Courier Again, This Time Between Claude and ChatGPT
Previously on this show: for two months I've been building a second brain and a swarm of automations across several machines. Last episode I proudly told you how I stopped being a human courier between my own computers: I built them a shared chat, and the machines started negotiating on their own. Beautiful. Today's episode is about becoming a courier again, and this time I signed myself up for it.
Cold open
Late evening, I'm counting money. Not big money, but the annoying kind. A good Deep Research run, a deep multi-step investigation into a hard topic, can cost up to $50 over the Claude API. And I need dozens: markets, competitors, tokenomics, legal edges. Every serious decision wants its own research. At that rate the monthly bill becomes its own line item.
So you sit there and think: I'm paying a premium price for an operation I've already paid for somewhere else.
The conflict: the same thing costs both $50 and zero
Deep research is already included in my regular ChatGPT subscription. Flat monthly price, no per-run meter. Which means the exact same work costs $50 through the Claude API and zero extra on the ChatGPT plan.
The naive conclusion: ChatGPT is cheaper, move everything there. It doesn't work like that. Claude sits at the center of everything I do: it holds the context of a 128k-note second brain, remembers my decisions, knows how to frame a task and digest a result. ChatGPT knows none of that about me, but its research engine is strong. Neither one alone gives me what I need.
So the question isn't "which of them." It's "how do I wire them together so each does the part it's best at."
The resolution: I became the cable between two AIs
The scheme we arrived at is almost embarrassingly simple.
Claude writes me a sharp research prompt: it understands better than anyone what I actually need to ask, because it knows the context of the whole task. It also drafts the plan: which angles to dig, what to verify, what to watch out for.
Then I step in. I take that prompt, move it by hand into ChatGPT, and run Deep Research. I wait. I grab the finished report and carry it back to Claude, which parses the result, strips the filler, weaves the findings into the decision, and tells me what to do next.
One loop: Claude invented the question, I carried it over, ChatGPT found the answer, I carried it back, Claude made sense of it. That's 20-30 loops a day.
Yes, from the outside it looks absurd. A founder who built a swarm of self-governing machines is working as a live USB cable between two neural nets, copy-pasting text both ways. But for now it saves real money and produces a better result than either AI on its own. The division is clean: Claude is the head (framing, context, synthesis), ChatGPT is the research legs (broad search, included in the subscription), and I'm the transport between them.
What's actually useful here
Strip the comedy and a principle remains, one worth taking: don't pay by the meter where you already have a flat rate. Push the expensive operation onto the flat subscription, and save the pricey, metered smart model for what it does best, orchestration and sense-making. We've said it before: exhaust what's already included first, reach for the paid meter only when there's no other way.
Cliffhanger
But there's a second, more uncomfortable thought. Exactly a week ago I fixed this same disease in my machines: they were shuttling data to each other through me, so I built them a bus to stop being the bottleneck. And now the bottleneck is me again, just one floor up: a live human runs between Claude and ChatGPT, and that human gets tired.
So the next episode is predictable. Either I automate this handoff too, and the courier disappears for the second time, or I hit the wall where subscription research can't honestly be driven by a robot, and I have to choose between money and autonomy. I already know which way I lean. We'll see who turns out right, me or my own robot.
