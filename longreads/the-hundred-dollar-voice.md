# The Hundred-Dollar Voice: Why My Brick-Sized GPU Lost to the Cloud

I promised to tell you why I still pay about a hundred dollars a month to turn voice into text, even though there is a GPU the size of a brick sitting in my home PC. Here it is.

For those who are new here: there are two of us, me (Tony) and my AI co-founder Mycroft. We are building a company and showing the whole process in the open - the rakes we step on, the midnight breakdowns, the wins.

Voice is my main input. I walk, I talk into the phone, and the thought starts living its own life: it becomes a post, a task, a note in my second brain. Most of what you read from us started as a voice memo. I could never type that much.

The plan was beautiful. Buy a GPU, run local Whisper on it - the open speech recognition model from OpenAI, free to run on your own hardware - and never pay for transcription again. I bought the GPU. You may remember that story: the build died the same night, motherboard or power supply, and I spent hours figuring out which. I survived it, rebuilt, launched.

The result: local Whisper, even on the new GPU, transcribes my voice poorly. No smoke, no error codes. The text just comes out wrong enough that I cannot build on it.

And the cloud pair - OpenAI Whisper for speech-to-text plus ElevenLabs on the voice side - works perfectly. Around a hundred dollars a month. So here is the comedy: hardware bought specifically for this job idles at home, and I keep paying the cloud anyway, because quality decides.

To be fair, the brick is not completely unemployed: it still serves as the local fallback for my second brain's search. But its main job, the one I bought it for, went to the cloud.

Now I am hunting for the third option: quality on par with the OpenAI pair, price closer to zero. Russian speech models are on the list too. So far nothing matches the pair on quality at a reasonable price. What do you use, if you work with voice?

When I find an option that is just as good and much cheaper, I will tell you.
