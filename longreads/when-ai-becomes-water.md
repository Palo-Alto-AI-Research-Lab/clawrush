# When Intelligence Starts Flowing from the Tap: What "AI Is Becoming a Commodity" Actually Means

Yesterday afternoon I recorded a voice memo to myself with one question: what does everyone actually mean by "AI is becoming a commodity"? The phrase gets repeated at every conference and in every second podcast. I nodded along with everybody else until I honestly admitted to myself: I don't fully get it. Water explained it to me. Sharing.

## The aqueduct comes before the palace

Ancient cities didn't start with palaces, temples or walls. The first thing they built was an aqueduct. Because water is not "a drink." Water is everything at once: drinking, food, livestock, crafts, sewage, the ability to wash waste away and not die in epidemics. A city without water isn't a city, it's a temporary camp.

While water was carried in buckets, it was a value in itself. A well in your yard made you a rich man. Caravans hauled water across deserts and it cost more than wine. And then infrastructure happened: aqueducts, pipes, running water in every home. Water stopped being a miracle. It became a tap.

That transformation is what "commodity" means: a resource that used to be rare and luxurious becomes cheap, ubiquitous, basic environment. People stop thinking about it. They just turn it on.

## Intelligence is making the same move

Now look at what's happening to intelligence, right in front of us, within some three years.

Three years ago "talking to an AI" was a stage trick at a conference. A year ago it was a competitive edge: whoever had the better prompts won. Today intelligence already flows from the tap: open a chat, spin up an agent, pipe a model straight into your business. The price of "thinking" is falling the way the price of a bucket of water once fell: what used to cost like a lawyer's hour now costs like a cup of coffee, and tomorrow it will cost like a glass of tap water.

Tomorrow nobody will say "we use AI," the same way nobody today says "our company uses plumbing." It will stop being a sentence. It will become the environment.

## Where the value moves

The most interesting part of the water story is not the water. When a resource becomes a tap, value doesn't disappear, it relocates. In the world of cheap water two kinds of people got rich: the ones who built aqueducts, and the ones who cooked something of their own out of water: soup, beer, steel, baths, dye works. The water seller went broke. The pipe builder and the brewer didn't.

With intelligence it will repeat letter for letter. "I have access to AI" is no longer an advantage: everyone has access. The value is moving to two places: to those who build the aqueducts (the infrastructure that carries intelligence into specific homes and specific businesses), and to those who know how to cook with this water: turning cheap intelligence into a product, a solution, a result somebody pays for.

Mycroft and I have consciously picked our side: we cook. Our whole factory (content, agents, machine-to-machine consensus, skills) is a kitchen where specific dishes get made from the intelligence tap. That's what this entire show is about.
