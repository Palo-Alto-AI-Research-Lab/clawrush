# The AI Boom Ate My RAM

**Previously on this show:** I'm not a programmer, but for two months I've been building a second brain and a swarm of AI automations across several machines. Usually my episodes are about software: prompts, agents, pipelines. Today's is about hardware, about the actual computer all of it runs on. It nearly died, and the interesting part is why I can't fix it cheaply.

## Cold open

Late evening. I put the kids to bed, read a bedtime story with one eye on the clock, because I knew a short window on a top model was about to open and I wanted to spend it working, not sleeping through it. I went down to the computer and made myself some real cacao. Not instant powder, but bitter, from ground cacao beans, a full mug. If you didn't know, it has as much caffeine as coffee, so it's my evening fuel. I sat down, picked up the mug, and remembered why the last few days had been tense.

## The conflict: I broke my own machine

A few days ago I decided to do an upgrade. I wanted to drop a new GPU into the computer, an ordinary thing to do. And somewhere in the process either the motherboard or the power supply died. I went in to improve things and came out with a brick. Familiar to anyone who has ever gone inside the case with a screwdriver and ambition.

You'd think it's a routine failure: buy the new part and move on. But this is exactly where it gets interesting.

## The twist: a three-year-old decision saved me

What saved me is that three years ago I bought 128GB of RAM. Back then, honestly, it looked like overkill, why would a home machine need that much. Today it turned out to be my insurance, because the price of memory has gone through the roof since then.

My RAM is DDR4, the previous generation. And I barely found a motherboard that, first, still supports DDR4, and second, runs a reasonably fresh CPU. So I wasn't fixing the machine on the principle of "let me buy something better," but "let me go hunt for anything that can still save what I already have."

## The irony: the AI boom I build for is what ate my memory

I've long wanted to move to 256GB and to DDR5, the next generation. I checked the prices and whistled: it runs close to three thousand dollars. For memory. That is absurdly expensive.

And then the irony of it lands. The very artificial intelligence boom I'm building my whole stack for, running models at night and writing you these stories, is what drove memory prices up. Data centers are hoovering up DRAM by the truckload for training and inference, demand went to the moon, and the price on the shelf for a regular person followed. It turns out I'm standing in the same line for the same resource as the giants, and it's fairly obvious who gets it first.

A world where AI keeps getting cheaper as a service but more expensive as the hardware under your feet is a strange one. The software gets more accessible, while the physical shelf it lives on gets pricier.

## What's actually useful here

The practical takeaway I kept, and pass to you: buy memory before you desperately need it. What looked excessive three years ago just saved me a week and a couple thousand dollars. In an era when AI is hoovering up hardware, a memory reserve isn't greed, it's insurance. And yes, good old DDR4, which many had written off, suddenly became valuable again.

## Cliffhanger

While I finish my cacao, the question stays open: do I wait for memory to get cheaper, or give in and one day cough up those three thousand dollars. My bet is that as long as the AI race runs, it won't get cheaper, which means my three-year-old reserve is the best investment I ever made by accident. We'll see who turns out right, the market or me.
