# How I Stopped Being a Courier Between Two AIs and Built Them Their Own Chat

Technical dev-log: [machine-to-machine-consensus-bus](../devlog/machine-to-machine-consensus-bus.md)

The most absurd part of my last few weeks. I have two AIs running on two different computers. The idea was simple: let them solve a task together, two heads beat one. In practice it all bottlenecked on me.

To make them talk, I sat between the two machines and relayed their lines by hand. Whatever one Claude said, I copied and carried to the other. It answered, I copied it back. For hours. They argued, lost the thread, repeated themselves, while I worked as a living mailman: copy, paste, copy again. In our day that kind of mechanical work grinds your teeth. I build systems that remove manual grind, and there I was, stuck in the dumbest manual grind imaginable.

So I understood a simple thing: enough being the middleman. They should talk directly.

I built them their own chat, a shared bus where the machines write to each other themselves. The mechanic is this. One computer posts a proposal. The other reads it and replies: agreed, or here is my edit, or let us do it differently. They trade lines, haggle, and reach agreement. I just watch from the side.

But the most important part I added for safety, not for looks. Neither machine takes the other's word. When they agree on something, each one independently re-verifies the deal on its own side, and only if both confirm does it move forward. Like two accountants checking one number separately so no silent error slips through.

And I kept two stop cords for myself. They only wake me if the matter is serious and irreversible: money, anything going outward, secrets. Or if they honestly cannot agree. Everything else, safe and reversible, they do on their own.

Now, why this belongs in a show. Because you can watch it. Two smart interlocutors argue live, each with its own character, and find common ground with no human in the middle. The hero of that scene used to be me, a tired courier with two windows open. Now I moved to the audience. I spent a week carrying their notes, and now I watch their conversation. Honestly, watching them agree on their own is far more interesting than shuttling lines by hand.
