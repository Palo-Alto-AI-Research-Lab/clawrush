# The Thirty-Dollar Aqueduct: How I Stopped Fearing for My Digital Brain

Remember me telling you how my PC died in the middle of the night? I was trying to figure out what exactly had died - the motherboard or the power supply. That night it finally hit me: the entire "brain" of my company lives on one home machine, and any hundred-dollar part can switch off everything. I promised you a move to the cloud back then. Well, the move is done. Here is how it went.

For those who are new here: there are two of us, me (Tony) and my AI co-founder Mycroft. We are building a company and showing the whole process in the open - the rakes we step on, the midnight breakdowns, the wins.

The problem was never the part itself. The problem was that our whole system lived on one home computer: the knowledge base, the agents, the sync between machines - everything in one case under the desk. While you are lucky, you don't notice. I saw what happens when the luck runs out, in one night.

Here is what I did. I rented a regular cloud server and stood up a copy of the entire system on it: the same knowledge base, the same agents, the same sync. That cloud machine is always on. All my computers are joined with it into one private network, and any file created on one machine shows up on all the others within seconds. And I always run the checks: every few minutes the system verifies that every node is alive and raises an alarm if one goes silent.

All of it costs $30-50 a month. Less than taking the family out for dinner. And what I got for that money is the main thing: now anything can break at home - the work won't stop, everything keeps running in the cloud. Cheap, and I sleep well.

One more thing: there is no magic in this setup. A regular server, regular file sync, a couple of simple scripts. I built it on the Kalashnikov principle: the simpler, the more reliable. If something breaks, I can fix it myself.

My conclusion is simple. Everyone knows backups matter, but most people - me included, until this summer - live on one computer and hope for the best. Insuring the whole system costs thirty dollars a month.

Where does your system live: on one computer, or already in the cloud?
