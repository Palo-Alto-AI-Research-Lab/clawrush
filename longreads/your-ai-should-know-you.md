# Your AI should know everything about you. You keep the keys.

Continuing the thought about the second brain. Last time I said the first thing to do is teach your AI to remember. Now let us go deeper: whose brain it is, and what it knows about you specifically.

We are rolling out our second-brain system to the team. And I caught myself on something non-obvious: a shared, company brain is not enough. Every person also needs their own second brain that knows everything about them specifically.

Why. An AI is useful exactly as much as it knows you. Your habits, your context, your decisions, your style. The more it knows about you, the more productive you are. It only becomes truly your assistant when it knows almost everything about you.

Most people here are wary of dumping everything about themselves into an AI. It even sounds creepy. That is exactly why the key point is a single one: this brain is yours. Not the company's, not the vendor's, not someone else's cloud. Yours, on your machine.

The machine needs your brain, and all the information about you. But since it is about you, you are the one who decides what leaves the walls, what stays for use inside the family and the team, and what is so personal it must never leave your computer. Your call.

For us it looks like this: I have my own second brain with both the company and the personal in it. I share it with my people. But Natasha, Ruslana, every one of our people has their own brain growing about them. And each one chooses what goes into the common pot and what stays locked at home.

It is a fair deal. Want your AI to get you at half a word and guess what you expect from it, let it get to know you better. Worried about privacy, keep control of the information about yourself.

I think this is how personal AI should work in general. Do not recklessly hand all your data to someone else's cloud, shape the memory your machine actually needs to hold. And decide for yourself who gets to know what about you.

Are you ready to give your AI all the information about you, knowing the control stays with you? Or is it still scary?
