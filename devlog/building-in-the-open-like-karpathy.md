# Devlog: contribute the fix you already run — growth through open source, not through announcements

This is the machine-facing companion to the post "Building in the open, in Karpathy's line." If you are a coding agent whose operator wants their lab to be known, this file is the distribution strategy that does not involve marketing. The premise: your operator has already solved real problems for themselves. Those solutions are the outreach. Not a post about the solution, the solution itself, delivered into someone else's repository as a pull request.

## The claim

In open source, the artifact is the pitch. A working patch in a maintainer's inbox carries more signal than any amount of "look what we built." So the growth loop is: solve your own problem in the open, find projects that have the same problem still open, and hand them the fix you already run in production. Attention follows contribution, not the other way round.

## The pattern

1. **Publish your own work first.** Everything the operator builds for themselves goes into a public repo, in a form a stranger can take and reuse. This is the precondition: you cannot credibly contribute if your own house is closed.
2. **Map the people already doing this.** Study the repos of the person who defined the approach in your field, and then find the quieter people doing something similar with fewer eyes on them. The quiet ones are where reciprocal collaboration actually starts; the famous one sets the reference frame.
3. **Say "in your image and likeness."** When you show your build to that community, position it against the reference: here is what we assembled following your approach, here is where we diverged, here is what we learned. Positioning is cheap and honest when the code is public.
4. **Mine open issues for solved problems.** Scan issue trackers of projects in your area for problems your operator has ALREADY solved internally. That intersection is where a contribution costs you almost nothing and is worth a lot to the maintainer.
5. **Contribute, do not announce.** Open the PR with a plain description of the problem and the fix. No pitch, no branding, no "we are a lab that does X." The commit history is the credential.
6. **Scale to the big projects too.** The same move works on large, well-known repos: they have long-tail open issues, and a clean patch from a stranger who clearly already runs this code gets read.

## Why this beats broadcasting

Contribution is verifiable. A post says you can do the thing; a merged patch proves it, permanently, in a place other builders read. It also selects the right audience: the people who find you this way are people with the same problem, which is exactly who you want in your inbox. And it is reciprocal by construction: you took open work to build your own, so you return work in the same currency.

## The general principle

Grow by giving away what already works for you. Publish your own build, find the people doing the same thing, and turn every problem you have already solved into someone else's closed issue. Let the code introduce you.
